Podcast

This podcast aims to comments on the “T” shaped student. While it is Important to be knowledgeable in your field, also having a range of knowledge helps you apply your skills in valuable ways.

We hyperbolized the character in this podcast in order to comment on the humanities vs science clash that often appears in society. To me, being T shaped means that in order to be the most successful, student, employee, etc., we have to be knowledgeable about our primary discipline. However, having broad knowledge allows us mindfully use what we know.

Often times in movies and literature you see this clash between the arts and the sciences. This phenomenon is popular in movies and literature because it is relevant to “real” life in more instances than not. We used our podcast to comment on how this phenomenon is prevalent at Virginia Tech, and how striving to be a little more “T” shaped would address this.

The podcast uses two colliding forces, the creator behind new technology, and a reviewer to comment on how being “T” shaped would make this situation work out for the best. The podcast largely speaks for itself. Often times when trying to decide of something should be all one way or another, the answer usually is found in the gray area. Our podcast comments on this by showing that, no, the technology must be priced in order to make some sort of profit (surprise, surprise, people are motivated by financial gains), but also there is room for products creator to consider alternative uses for the technology.

Our video focuses on how the “V” can create a sense of community and supports both the vertical and horizontal lines of the “T”.

Through this video, my group and I discuss our experiences at Virginia Tech to-date, and how we feel that they are “V” shaped. There are many opportunities at this university to be involved beyond the classroom. Many students seek out these experiences as the experiences make them better students and more prepared for the ever-looming Real World.

CollaborationSpace

Here’s what I’ve gotten from the the VT-shaped model: Our world is becoming multifaceted, and in order to “keep up” we must make ourselves as interdisciplinary as possible. However, this does not suggest that we must be all be English language experts and scientists and accountants all at the same time. As engineers or as english students, we need each other. In order to be successful and make the most out of our time and our careers, it’s important to know when to ask for help. Cue CollaborationSpace!.

CollaborationSpace is a website/app that I have proposed in response to the VT-shaped model for this final. It is largely based the idea of TimeBanks. If you haven’t heard it of the TimeBank before, it a Craigslist–esq service that connects people who have skills (for example, shelf-building) to people that need assistance (for example, someone who needs a shelf built). The website connects the two, and the shelf-builder is paid with “hours” that the other person as accumulated from providing a service or skills to someone else. For more information about TimeBanks click here.

Home Page

This is the homepage of the website and of the app. After the user logs into their account, they can see all of the projects rejects that are currently available, with a short name and description. This looks similar to any other online shopping website. In order to learn more about the project, users can simply click on it.

Project Details

This brings them to the project details page. Here, the user can find out more about what the project entails: what needs to be done, how long it is expected to take, etc. Here the user can message other users and ask questions, agree on parameters of the project, and commit to help one another.

My Profile

Before a user accepts help, they can view other’s profiles. Each user’s profile details the user’s skills, past projects, reviews from other users, and picture. This allows the user to get to know the other’s skills a little bit before they begin working together.

I believe that this website helps address the VT-shaped model in that is allows members of the Virginia Tech community to use their skills to help others out. This not only allows students to practice their editing, or engineering skills in practical, low-pressure ways, but students working together to produce better products strengthen the community and simulate real-world collaboration.
